Beginner Guide to Online Payment Safety in Kenya

Sending money from your phone has never been easier—but it has also never been easier for scammers to trick you. This beginner guide will show you practical, Kenyan‑friendly habits you can use to keep your money safe whenever you pay online.

Many Kenyans have a painful story about sending money to the wrong person: a fake shop on Instagram, a “friend of a friend” selling a phone, or a stranger promising double returns. In most cases, the victim had good intentions—they simply did not know the right questions to ask before pressing “Send”.

Good online payment safety is not about being paranoid. It is about having a small checklist in your head that you run through every time you are about to pay. Once it becomes a habit, you can trade confidently without fearing every single deal.

1. Understand the Main Ways You Pay Online

In Kenya today, most everyday online payments happen through a few main channels:

  • M-PESA: Paybill, till numbers, and direct phone‑to‑phone transfers.
  • Bank cards: Debit or credit cards used on websites or apps.
  • Bank or wallet transfers: Sending directly from your banking app or fintech wallet.

Each method has its own risks. For example, card payments can expose your card number if the site is fake, while direct M‑PESA transfers can go to scammers who never deliver. Your goal is to reduce risk, whichever channel you are using.

2. Golden Rule: Always Slow Down Before You Pay

Scammers rely on speed and pressure. They want you to act before you think:

  • “Offer ends in 10 minutes, send now!”
  • “Someone else is ready to pay, are you serious or not?”
  • “If you delay, I will sell to another person.”

A simple safety hack is this: whenever you feel rushed, pause for at least one full minute. Re‑read the messages. Ask yourself, “If I never see this money again, can I live with it?” If the answer is no, you must demand more proof or move to safer options like escrow.

Legitimate businesses rarely threaten you into paying immediately. Pressure and secrecy are classic scam signals.

3. Verify the Person, Not Just the Item

Beginners often focus only on the product: Is the phone genuine? Is the laptop the right model? But online safety also depends on who you are dealing with.

Basic verification steps include:

  • Check profile history: How old is their account? Do they post real‑life content or only ads?
  • Ask for a short voice note or call: Scammers often avoid real‑time calls.
  • Search their phone number online: Many victims warn others by posting numbers used in scams.
  • Be careful with “new accounts”: A brand‑new profile selling high‑value items cheaply is a major red flag.
Example: Instagram shop selling sneakers

You find a page with nice sneaker photos, all posted within the last two weeks. No tagged customers, no reviews, no videos, no physical shop details. Prices are far below normal market prices. Even if the product looks good, the profile story is weak. It is safer to walk away or insist on escrow and stronger verification.

4. Use Official Payment Channels—Not Random Personal Numbers

When paying a business, always ask yourself, “Is this payment method traceable and consistent with a real business?” Safer signs include:

  • An official Paybill or till number that appears on their website or business card.
  • Receipts or confirmations that match the business name, not just a random personal name.
  • Clear written instructions that remain the same every time you talk to them.

Be very careful when:

  • The seller keeps changing the number “because that line has an issue”.
  • They refuse to share any registered business details.
  • They tell you to “just send to this cousin, it is the same thing”.

Each extra layer of confusion around where the money is going increases your risk.

5. Combine Escrow with Good Habits

Escrow (like TrustPay KE) is one of the strongest safety tools you can use, but it works best when paired with good habits:

  • Agree in writing: Use WhatsApp or email to record item, price, delivery and timelines.
  • Share clear evidence: Photos, videos and receipts help if a dispute arises.
  • Use the official process: Create an order properly instead of “sending on the side”.
  • Respect the dispute window: Inspect goods quickly and raise issues within the allowed time.

6. Protect Your Phone Like a Wallet

Many online payment “hacks” actually start with stolen phones or SIM cards. Protecting your device is the first wall of defence:

  • Always use a strong screen lock (PIN, pattern, fingerprint or face ID).
  • Do not save your M‑PESA PIN or card details in plain text in Notes or screenshots.
  • Enable SIM lock if you are comfortable managing the PIN.
  • Log out of financial apps on shared or borrowed devices.
Example: Lost phone at a matatu stage

If your phone has no screen lock and your M‑PESA PIN is saved in a text message, a thief only needs a few minutes to drain your accounts and approve new digital loans. A strong lock and good PIN hygiene can be the difference between inconvenience and a financial disaster.

7. Simple Checklist Before You Tap “Send”

Before any online payment, quickly ask yourself:

  • 1. Do I really know who I am paying? (Not just their username, but a real person or business.)
  • 2. Is the amount correct? Double‑check zeros and decimal points.
  • 3. Am I using an official, verified payment channel?
  • 4. Do I have screenshots of our agreement and the product/service?
  • 5. Would I still feel safe if this deal is delayed by a few days?

If any answer worries you, pause and either gather more information or suggest using escrow for extra safety.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

1. Is it safe to screenshot and send my M‑PESA statement to sellers?

Only share what is absolutely necessary. Full statements can expose your phone number, balances and where you shop, which criminals can use for social engineering. If a seller needs proof of payment, a normal transaction SMS screenshot with sensitive parts blurred is usually enough.

2. Can I reverse a payment if I realise I was scammed?

Reversals are not guaranteed, especially if the scammer withdraws quickly. You should try:

  • Using the official reversal options provided by your payment channel immediately.
  • Contacting customer care with full details.
  • Reporting to relevant authorities if the amount is large.

However, prevention is much more reliable than hoping for a reversal after the fact.

3. Should I ever share my PIN with someone I trust?

No. Never share your M‑PESA, bank or card PIN with anyone, even close family or friends. If someone truly needs to help you (for example, an elderly relative), set up proper joint solutions instead of giving away your secret PIN.

4. Is paying on delivery (COD) safer than paying online?

Cash on delivery can reduce some risks, but it also has its own problems: fake items delivered in a hurry, pressure to pay before proper testing, or couriers who disappear with both item and cash. A well‑structured escrow process with clear inspection steps is usually safer than informal COD with strangers.

5. How often should I review my payment safety habits?

At least a few times a year, take 10–15 minutes to:

  • Update passwords and PINs where necessary.
  • Review which apps are connected to your M‑PESA or bank.
  • Read or watch a recent resource on new scam tactics targeting Kenyans.

Treat online payment safety like servicing a car—it is cheaper and easier to maintain good habits than to repair damage after an incident.
