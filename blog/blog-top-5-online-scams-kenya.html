Top 5 Online Scams in Kenya and How to Avoid Them

Stay safe by knowing the common tricks scammers use and how to protect yourself from fraud.

As online trading grows in Kenya, so do the scams. Knowing the common tricks is your best defense. The more familiar you are with how fraudsters think, the easier it becomes to pause, ask the right questions, and walk away from dangerous deals before money leaves your account.

1. Fake Online Stores

Scammers create fake websites or social media pages that look legitimate, advertise popular products at too-good-to-be-true prices, take payments, then disappear. Many of these pages borrow photos from genuine shops, copy logos and colour schemes, and even run paid ads to appear trustworthy.

How to protect yourself: Search for independent reviews, check how long the page has been active, look for tagged customer photos, and avoid sellers who refuse meetups or escrow for higher-value items.

2. Advance Fee Fraud

A "seller" asks for a deposit or advance payment to secure a deal, then never delivers. Common in car, land, and rental scams, this trick preys on fear of missing out: “If you don’t send something now, I will give it to the next buyer.” Once you pay, the story changes or the person disappears.

How to protect yourself: Refuse to pay deposits to personal numbers without a contract and clear protection. For big items like cars, land and rentals, use escrow or pay only after a legitimate viewing and paperwork check.

3. Counterfeit Products

Buyers pay for genuine products but receive cheap imitations. Very common with electronics, designer items, and pharmaceuticals, these fakes may look convincing in photos but fail quickly in real use. Some sellers mix a few genuine items with fakes to confuse reviews.

How to protect yourself: Learn basic checks for the specific product (IMEI for phones, serial numbers for electronics, packaging checks for cosmetics and medicine) and prefer verified shops or escrow-backed deals for expensive purchases.

4. Freelancer Scams

Clients hire freelancers, receive work, then refuse to pay. Or freelancers take payment upfront and never deliver the work. In both directions, lack of structure and written agreement makes it easy for one side to disappear once they get what they want.

How to protect yourself: Use written scopes, milestones, and escrow for payments. Clients should only release funds after confirming deliverables; freelancers should avoid doing full projects with no payment protection.

5. Romance Scams

Scammers build online relationships, then ask for money for emergencies, medical bills, or travel. They vanish once paid. These scams can stretch over weeks or months as emotional trust is slowly built before money is requested.

How to protect yourself: Be very cautious about sending money to people you have never met in person, especially if they avoid video calls or clear identification. Genuine partners will respect your boundaries when you say you are not comfortable sending money.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

1. What is the single biggest red flag that an online deal is a scam?

The strongest red flag is a combination of very low price, high pressure, and refusal to provide proof. If a seller gets angry when you ask for verification or suggest safer payment methods, that is usually your cue to leave.

2. Are all sellers on social media risky?

No. Many genuine Kenyan businesses operate fully on social media. The risk comes when there is no history, no real customer feedback, no willingness to meet or use escrow, and constant rush. Look at the whole picture, not just the platform.

3. How can I introduce escrow into a conversation without offending the other person?

You can frame escrow as protection for both of you: “I like using escrow because it protects me from losing money and also guarantees that serious buyers do not waste your time. Can we use TrustPay KE so that the money is held safely until everything is confirmed?” Genuine traders usually see this as a professional step, not an insult.
