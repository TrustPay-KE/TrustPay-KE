Beginner Escrow Guide for Kenyans: How It Actually Works

If you are new to online buying or selling in Kenya, the word “escrow” can feel complicated and foreign. In reality, escrow is a very simple idea: a trusted middle person safely holds money until everyone has done what they agreed to do. This guide explains escrow in clear, Kenyan English with realistic examples you can relate to.

Every day, Kenyans lose money to fake Instagram shops, ghost freelancers and “too good to be true” marketplace deals. In most of these stories, the money moves directly from buyer to seller with no protection in between. If something goes wrong, it becomes a long, painful chase.

Escrow flips that script. Instead of trusting a stranger with your hard‑earned cash, you trust a neutral third party to hold the money safely until the transaction is complete. If you understand this one idea, you already understand 70% of escrow.

What Is Escrow in Simple Kenyan Terms?

In simple words, escrow is a safe lock box for your money during a trade. The buyer sends money to the escrow account, not to the seller. The seller only receives the money after the buyer confirms that the item or service is as agreed.

Example: Buying a phone from a stranger

A seller on Instagram is offering a phone for KES 18,000. Instead of sending money directly to their personal M‑PESA number, you:

  • Agree on price and terms with the seller.
  • Send the KES 18,000 to the escrow account.
  • The escrow service confirms they have received the funds.
  • The seller delivers the phone.
  • You check it, test it, and confirm you are happy.
  • Only then does the escrow service release the money to the seller.

If the seller never delivers, or sends a fake item, the money is still in the lock box. You can raise a dispute before funds are released.

The Basic Escrow Flow in Kenya

While different platforms have small variations, most escrow transactions in Kenya follow five key steps:

  1. Agree: Buyer and seller agree on item, price, delivery method and timelines.
  2. Deposit: Buyer sends money to the official escrow account (not to the seller).
  3. Confirm: Escrow confirms that payment is received and informs both parties.
  4. Deliver: Seller delivers the product or completes the service.
  5. Release: Buyer confirms everything is okay, then escrow releases funds to the seller.

The powerful part is this: no one holds both the money and the item at the same time until the deal is closed. That is what removes most of the risk.

Why Escrow Matters So Much in Kenya

Kenya is a mobile‑first country. It is normal to:

  • Order goods from Jiji or Facebook Marketplace sellers you have never met.
  • Hire freelancers through WhatsApp DMs or Telegram groups.
  • Pay rent deposits or booking fees via M‑PESA to a landlord or agent.

All of these situations involve high trust but low protection when you pay directly. Escrow adds structure and accountability without making the process too technical for ordinary Kenyans.

Think of escrow as that wise, trusted “msee wa mtaa” who says, “Weka hiyo doo kwa mimi kwanza, then once everything is ok, I will give them.” Only here, the “msee” is a formal platform with clear rules.

Key People Involved in an Escrow Transaction

A typical escrow transaction has three main parties:

  • Buyer: The person sending money and receiving the goods or service.
  • Seller: The person receiving money and providing the goods or service.
  • Escrow Provider (like TrustPay KE): The neutral middle party holding funds and enforcing the process.

The escrow provider is not working on the side of the buyer or the seller. Their job is to follow the agreed rules and make sure nobody skips important steps.

When Should a Beginner Use Escrow?

As a Kenyan beginner, a good rule is:

If you cannot afford to lose the money, or you do not fully trust the other party, use escrow.

Typical situations where escrow makes sense include:

  • Buying phones, laptops or electronics from social media or classifieds.
  • Paying large deposits for cars, land, rent or furniture.
  • Hiring freelancers for design, websites, marketing or software work.
  • Any deal where delivery and payment do not happen at the exact same time.

Simple Beginner Example: Paying a Freelancer

Imagine you hire a graphic designer from Twitter to create a logo for your new side hustle. You agree on KES 6,000 for the work. Both of you are worried:

  • You do not want to pay in full then get ghosted.
  • The freelancer does not want to design for hours then be told, “We will pay you later.”
How escrow solves this
  1. You and the freelancer agree on a clear scope and deadline.
  2. You start an escrow order for KES 6,000.
  3. You send the KES 6,000 to the escrow account via M‑PESA.
  4. Escrow confirms to both of you that the funds are safely held.
  5. The freelancer does the work and delivers the logo files.
  6. You review the work and request small changes if needed.
  7. Once you are happy, you confirm in escrow and the freelancer is paid.

Both sides can relax a bit more, because the money is not floating somewhere in the middle with no rules.

Common Beginner Fears About Escrow (and the Truth)

“Is escrow a scam itself?”

Any tool can be abused, but a genuine escrow platform will:

  • Use a clearly communicated business name and official payment details.
  • Have written Terms of Service, Privacy Policy and Dispute Policy.
  • Explain exactly how fees work and who pays them.
  • Provide support contacts that actually respond.

If a “so‑called escrow” is just a random personal M‑PESA number with no structure or documentation, treat it as a red flag.

“Will the seller refuse escrow because they think it’s slow?”

Some sellers simply have not used escrow before. You can explain that:

  • Funds are confirmed quickly once you pay.
  • They can see proof that money is already set aside for them.
  • They avoid fake payment screenshots and bounced promises.

Many serious sellers actually prefer escrow once they understand that it protects them as well.

Beginner Checklist Before You Start Escrow

Before sending any money into escrow, run through this quick checklist:

  • 1. Confirm the real buyer/seller: Use a phone call or video call if possible, and check their profile history.
  • 2. Agree on details in writing: Item description, condition, price, delivery, and timelines.
  • 3. Confirm official escrow details: Paybill or till, account name and number, and reference format from the platform itself.
  • 4. Keep screenshots: Conversation history, payment confirmations and delivery proof.
  • 5. Understand the dispute window: Know how long you have to raise a complaint before funds are released.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

1. Do I need a bank account to use escrow, or is M‑PESA enough?

For most everyday Kenyan transactions, M‑PESA is enough. You do not need a bank account to use escrow. You simply send money from your M‑PESA line to the official escrow Paybill or till the platform provides. Always double‑check the number from official channels before paying.

2. Who pays the escrow fee—the buyer or the seller?

Different platforms handle this differently. Some split fees, some charge the buyer, and some charge the seller. On TrustPay KE, the fee structure and who pays is clearly shown before you confirm the order. The key is to discuss and agree in advance so there are no surprises.

3. What happens if the seller never delivers anything?

If the seller fails to deliver within the agreed time, you should raise a dispute within the dispute window. The escrow provider will review the evidence from both sides. If it is clear that nothing was delivered, the money is normally refunded to the buyer minus any applicable fees as explained in the dispute policy.

4. Can escrow protect me from every type of scam?

No system can remove 100% of risk. However, proper escrow:

  • Makes it much harder for someone to disappear with your money without delivering.
  • Creates a clear process and record of the deal.
  • Gives you a neutral party to review evidence if something goes wrong.

You still need common sense: avoid obviously fake deals, never share your PIN, and only use official payment details.

5. I am a seller. How does escrow help me?

As a seller, escrow assures you that the buyer has already set money aside for the transaction. You do not have to worry about fake screenshots, “I will pay when I get paid” stories, or last‑minute price changes after you have already delivered. Serious buyers who agree to escrow usually waste less of your time.
